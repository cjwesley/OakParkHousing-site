The Evidence Nº1 · The Questions

New construction is all luxury. How does that help affordability?

Oak Park built 50 affordable units. It gained 1,350. The market did the rest.

The 30-second answer

Since 2012, Oak Park approved 1,751 multifamily units. Only 50 were designated affordable. Over that same window, IHDA — the state agency that measures affordability for every Illinois municipality — counted Oak Park's affordable stock growing from 3,991 to 5,341 units. You cannot create a 1,350-unit increase with 50 designated units. It's impossible. New construction absorbed premium demand, and older stock became affordable. That's filtering, measured independently, in Oak Park.

The reason I trust filtering in Oak Park specifically isn't a theoretical argument. It's that our own affordable-housing stock grew by more than 1,350 units over a decade of new construction — measured by IHDA, not by me, on the same methodology it applies to every municipality in Illinois.

Here's the math nobody has answered. The Village's own housing report documents 1,751 multifamily units built since 2012, of which 50 are designated affordable. That 50 already contains every unit anyone tries to attribute the gains to — the IHO mandates, Van Buren, the Housing Authority development on Austin. Fifty units cannot produce a 1,350-unit increase. The other 1,300 became affordable the way housing always has: new construction absorbed the premium demand that was bidding up older stock, and older stock filtered down.

What filtering doesn't solve: housing for households below the bottom of the market. That requires direct subsidy, and I've never argued otherwise. The argument is that filtering plus direct subsidy beats direct subsidy alone — and the 27:1 ratio is what "beats" looks like in Oak Park's own numbers.
